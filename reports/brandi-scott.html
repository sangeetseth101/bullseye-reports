Their Story Before They Find You

Problem · Promise · Transformation

Show your audience that you understand where they are now — and can help them get where they want to go.

01
🔍
The Problem
Another Training Won't Fix This
She's leading a staff through constant change — shrinking budgets, turnover, internal conflict, and a steady stream of state and federal policy shifts that ripple straight down to her team. She's already booked the trainings. Sometimes the same one twice, because it "didn't quite land" the first time. She's carrying her division's piece of the institution's mission and feels the weight of it daily, but she can't quite name what's actually breaking down — only that if she doesn't fix it, more people walk, and the team that's left gets stretched even thinner.
02
✦
Your Promise
A Layered Plan, Not Another Workshop
You sit down with her — sometimes with her whole leadership team — and listen first. You peel back the layers until you find what's actually underneath the request, not just what was asked for. From there, you build a plan that's layered: individual coaching for her, tailored development for her team, and assessment built in before, during, and after the work — so she leaves not with a one-time fix, but with a roadmap she can keep using long after you're gone.
03
◎
The Transformation
From Reactive to Rooted
Across every engagement, the same shift happens. Leaders stop reacting to the latest crisis and start seeing the pattern underneath it — and they get honest about which parts of it are actually theirs to fix. Their teams gain the language to name what they need and ask for it directly. And the leader walks away with clarity on her own gaps, a plan to close them, and the confidence to lead from her own values instead of from whatever fire is loudest that week.

Who She Is
She's a director or senior leader on a college or university campus, overseeing a team of staff who directly support student outcomes and experiences. She's responsible for carrying her division's mission forward and aligning it with the institution's broader mission — which means she's constantly translating "big picture" pressure into something her team can actually act on. Underneath the title, she's navigating constant change, shrinking or shifting resources, turnover she didn't choose, and conflict between staff members she now has to manage. She feels the weight of her institution's mission and the federal and state policy changes reshaping higher education, but privately, she's not sure how to keep motivating a team that's already stretched thin — and she's quietly afraid that if she gets this wrong, the people she's worked hardest to support will be the ones who leave.

The BullsEye Formula™

Your BullsEye Brand Audit

Four pillars. Where your brand is already working — and where the real opportunity lives.

Strong
This is working. Your brand is clear and confident here. Keep building on it — this is where you have real authority.
Sharpening
This has real potential. You have something here — it just needs to be named, clarified, or made more visible. This is your biggest growth opportunity.
Pillar 01
Specialize
Claim the expertise you want to be known for.
Strong
"The specific area of expertise I'm well known for is helping higher education leaders build the communication, collaboration, and prioritization skills their teams need to navigate constant change. When people think of systemic leadership development for college and university teams, they think of me."
  • A PhD in higher education leadership, plus 15+ years as a senior leader who has personally led teams through organizational change, turnover, and shifting state and federal funding.
  • Every part of the offer — individualized coaching for the leader, tailored professional development for the team, and pre/mid/post assessment — is built around this one focus, not scattered across unrelated services.
Pillar 02
Specify
Define who you serve best and what they need most.
Strong
"I work primarily with directors and senior leaders on college campuses who struggle with leading their teams through constant change, turnover, and uncertainty, and who desire the confidence and clarity to carry their mission forward without burning out the staff who depend on them."
  • The audience is named precisely — directors and senior leaders overseeing teams that support student outcomes, accountable for aligning their division's mission with the institution's.
  • The internal struggle is named, too: the fear that unaddressed communication and collaboration issues will drive more turnover and overwhelm a team that's already stretched.
Pillar 03
Stand Out
Turn your difference into a memorable reason to choose you.
Sharpening
"Unlike other higher education consultants, people choose to work with Brandi Scott PhD LLC because we are unmatched in pairing real senior-leadership experience with research into the systemic challenges driving these problems — and because Brandi will ask a leader to look at her own role in the problem, not just her team's."
  • The differentiators are real and rare — first-generation graduate to senior higher ed leader to PhD researcher — but they're not yet named as a single, ownable story or method.
  • Testimonials consistently describe the same thing — patient but accountability-holding, theory translated into practice for real political and interpersonal dynamics — which is the proof a sharper claim could be built on.
Pillar 04
Solve
Package your process so people understand how you create results.
Sharpening
"We help our clients move from reacting to whatever training was just requested, to understanding the real, root-cause issue underneath it, with a layered coaching, development, and assessment process — so they walk away with a plan that builds lasting skills instead of a one-time fix."
  • The root cause she owns is real and specific — what looks like a skills gap (like a pronoun training request) is often a deeper trust and communication gap on the team.
  • The process itself — individual coaching, tailored PD, and pre/mid/post assessment with a final roadmap — is already repeatable. It just doesn't have a name yet, which makes it hard for a client to ask for by name or refer.

Your Point of View
The Uncomfortable Truth
Most higher ed leadership development treats communication problems like a training gap — book a workshop, check the box, move on. But the real problem is rarely the topic on the agenda. It's whether the team trusts each other enough to talk when something actually goes wrong. Leaders who skip that question keep buying the same training, year after year, for a problem it was never built to fix.
Your Differentiation
Why Only You
Brandi has personally led higher education teams through the exact pressures her clients are facing now — organizational change, staff turnover, and funding shifts driven by state and federal policy. She pairs that lived experience with a PhD in higher education leadership focused on the systemic challenges shaping these dynamics. Her own first-generation story gives her a direct line into the self-doubt that quietly holds capable leaders back, and she won't let a client hide behind "this is out of my control" without checking whether that's actually true. No one else bringing this work onto a college campus has sat in the senior leader's chair, studied the system from the inside, and is willing to hand the mirror back to the leader in the room.
Your Zone of Genius
What You Own
What Brandi hands a client goes beyond the workshop itself: a layered roadmap built from individual coaching for the leader, tailored development for their team, and assessment woven in from before the engagement starts to after it ends — so the leader has a way to keep building long after the work together is done.
Area to Consider
Your Website Doesn't Say What You Just Told Me
Right now, brandiscottphd.com introduces you as a "Communication and Leadership Consultant for Higher Education and NonProfits" who "specialize[s] in designing professional development experiences that meet the needs of each team and organization." That sentence could belong to almost any consultant working with colleges and nonprofits — it doesn't say what makes you different. It doesn't mention the thing you're actually best at: finding the real issue underneath a request, like a pronoun-training ask that turned out to be about trust on the team. It doesn't mention your layered coaching-plus-assessment process, or the lived experience and willingness to challenge a leader that your clients clearly value. The credential is on the site. The leadership behind it isn't yet.

What to Do This Month

Your Quick Wins

Your quick wins — simple actions you can take this month to build real momentum for your brand.

Week 1
Rewrite Your Homepage Headline
Replace "Communication and Leadership Consultant for Higher Education and NonProfits" with language that names what you actually do: find and fix the root cause beneath your team's communication breakdowns. The PhD and the lived experience are already there — your headline just needs to catch up.
Week 1
Add the Pronoun-Training Story to Your About Page
Without naming anyone, add a short version of the story where a "pronoun training" request turned out to be a team trust issue. It's the clearest example you have of how you diagnose what's really going on — and it's exactly the moment your ideal client will recognize from her own team.
Week 2
Name Your Process
Give your layered approach — individual coaching, tailored professional development, and pre/mid/post assessment with a final roadmap — an actual name. Right now it's described in steps. Once it has a name, it becomes something a client can ask for, and refer to a colleague.
Week 3
Write the "Mirror Moment" Piece
Publish the content piece built around "this is out of my control" — the moment you challenge a leader to check whether her own unclear expectations are driving the team's burnout. This is the hardest-to-copy thing about how you work, and right now it only lives in conversations.
Week 4
Build a Signature Talk Around the First-Gen Leader Story
Your background as a first-generation graduate, and the self-doubt work you do with women leaders, is a distinct asset nobody else in this space can claim. Turn it into a signature talk, keynote, or workshop that institutions can book by name — a personal story made into a repeatable, sellable offer.
